Your motorcycle needs care

Your motorcycle is a machine, and it needs consistent maintenance to work its best. Whether that be regular oil changes, brake replacements, engine work, tire changes or proper winterization. On this page, I’ll give some of my tips for some simple maintenance tasks to make them as easy as possible, so you can focus on what matters most, riding.

Oil change

Regular oil changes are one of the simplest and most effective ways to keep your motorcycle running smoothly. Before draining anything, always warm up the engine for a few minutes. Heating the oil helps it thin out, allowing it to flow more easily and carry out more contaminants when you remove the drain plug. When the engine is warm, place a drain pan under the bike and take your time removing the plug so you don’t strip the threads. Make sure to replace the crush washer every time you change the oil — it’s a cheap part that prevents leaks and ensures a tight seal, but many riders forget about it.

After installing a new filter and adding the correct amount of fresh oil, start the engine briefly and check for drips around the plug and filter. As for frequency, most motorcycles need an oil change roughly every 5,000 to 6,000 kilometers, but this varies by engine type, oil grade, and riding style. If you ride hard, take frequent short trips, or own an older engine, changing it more often is usually worth it. Keeping clean oil in the bike is one of the best ways to extend engine life and keep your rides smooth and reliable.

Brake replacements

Replacing your motorcycle’s brake pads is straightforward, but paying attention to the details makes a big difference in braking performance and safety. Before installing new pads, always inspect the caliper thoroughly. Make sure the pistons move smoothly and check for any rust or corrosion on their surfaces — buildup here can cause uneven pad wear or sticking brakes. If the pistons are dirty, clean them carefully before pushing them back into the caliper.

When fitting the new pads, ensure they sit properly in the retaining clips and that all sliding pins are clean and lightly greased. After reassembly, pump the brake lever a few times to seat the pads against the disc. It’s also a good idea to check the brake fluid level and condition during the process. With everything in good shape, your brakes will feel sharper, more consistent, and far safer on the road.

Winterization

Preparing your motorcycle for winter storage helps prevent headaches when riding season returns. Start by taking care of the battery, since it’s one of the first components to suffer during long periods of inactivity. Either remove it and store it indoors on a smart charger or keep it connected to a tender while the bike sits. For fuel-injected bikes, add a quality fuel stabilizer to the tank and let the engine run briefly so the additive circulates through the system. For carbureted motorcycles, it’s often best to drain the carbs completely to prevent varnish buildup or clogged jets in spring.

Give the chain a proper clean and lube, inflate the tires to the correct pressure, and ideally store the bike on a stand to avoid flat spots. A breathable cover will protect everything from dust without trapping moisture. With the battery maintained, the fuel treated, and the carbs drained where needed, your motorcycle will be ready to fire up smoothly when warmer days return.
